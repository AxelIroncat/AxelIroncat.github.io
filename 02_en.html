Chapter 2. The Formula of Power

Max stepped into the room, following Professor Blinov.

The warm air was infused with the sharp aroma of black tea, old books, and a faint trace of dust. Oval armchairs arranged around a slightly worn table created an atmosphere of intimate comfort—a place where people who had known each other for years gathered without ceremony. A large screen hung on the wall in the corner, silently broadcasting the news. Four professors sat at the table—familiar faces before whom Max had once sat for exams and defended term papers.

“Colleagues,” Professor Blinov said calmly, stopping beside the table, “allow me to introduce my assistant, Maxim.”

The conversation faded for a moment. For several seconds, they simply looked at him. Max felt his cheeks burn under their scrutiny.

The tall, dark-skinned Laurent Vignon was the first to break the pause. His deep voice, with a slight French accent, sounded playful.

“Maxim,” he drawled with barely concealed irony, narrowing his shrewd eyes. He wore a slightly worn gray jacket, the shoulders beginning to sag with time. “Now I feel old. I remember you as a bold student—a real handful: ambitious, self-assured, and, unfortunately, right in all your arguments.”

Max gave a slight nod, trying to contain his excitement.

“Thank you.”

Beside him, Tkach chuckled loudly. His massive frame, broad shoulders dusted with silver stubble, radiated strength. He leaned heavily on the table.

“You’re still holding a high bar,” Tkach said in an even but confident voice. “With that kind of potential, I honestly thought you’d go into a corporation. In a couple of years, you’d have built a career better than any of ours…”

“Not right away,” Anna Morozova interrupted him. Her slender figure and fine features behind thin-framed glasses seemed charged with nervous energy; her hands, adorned with a delicate ring, gripped the handle of her cup. “But I agree: you had potential. We were even surprised that you decided to stay here.”

Max felt the slight tension in his shoulders give way to steady confidence.

“Staying here is a conscious choice,” he replied with a faint smile. “I know I still have much to learn.”

“And the right one,” Blinov confirmed briefly. “I’ve been watching him for a long time. Now he’s where he should be.”

Vignon raised his teacup in a light toast.

“Then welcome,” he said. “You’re now in our small, capricious, eternally debating circle. Have a seat.”

Max took a place by the window. In the room’s dim light, cups clinked against saucers, the air filled with the invigorating aroma of tea. The conversation resumed, now as among colleagues: stories from graduate school, discussions of seminars, thoughtful silences. A quiet unease stirred in Max’s chest—he was sitting at the table for the first time not as a student, but as an equal, though still silent.

At that moment, the screen on the wall suddenly came to life. Anna Morozova instinctively raised her head, frowning.

“Something urgent…” she murmured, without lifting her cup. Her fingers reached for the remote and pressed a button. The volume immediately increased.

On the screen appeared a black background with large letters: “Emergency Statement from the Kairo Corporation.” The room went still. Tkach set down his cup, Vignon leaned forward, as if trying to see the image from a new angle. His eyes reflected the screen's glints.

She appeared on the screen. She didn’t walk to the podium or look around—she was already there. As if her presence had preceded time itself, and now the world had only to follow her shadow. Almost motionless, perfectly calibrated. Dark hair—not a strand out of place, part of her meticulously crafted image. No accessories, no jewelry, no flaws. For a moment, Max felt his heart skip: there was something elusive in her—not just beauty, not visible strength, but an almost singular precision bordering on the unnatural. It seemed she hadn’t been born, but designed. Every feature obeyed some unknown algorithm.

For a moment, it seemed her dark eyes looked straight into the lens—and then, as if straight at him. As if she knew that right here, in this corner of the world, he was. As if she had already decided what role to assign him in the future. Max gripped his cup tighter.

The scrolling ticker read: “Iris Tanaka, Chairwoman of the Board of the Kairo Corporation.” Impeccably composed, in a tailored, minimalist suit, with perfect makeup, she spoke in an even, calm voice. Her tone was soft, but each word fell exactly into place, allowing no objections.

“Over the past decades, the Kairo Corporation has become a key element of technological and economic infrastructure in many countries,” her words flowed over the silent faces. “We support critical processes: from logistics and medical networks to neuroanalytics, distributed computing, and decision-making systems. We don't just maintain infrastructure—we help it develop, synchronize, adapt to changing conditions. Today, I can confidently say: we are entering a new phase. The cycle of research begun by my father has reached maturity. A series of key experiments in neuroengineering have been completed: the first generation of implants capable of significantly enhancing cognitive processing efficiency is ready for deployment. This is direct support for those who face complex decisions under high uncertainty every day. And this is just the first step toward a deeper architecture of human-system interaction.”

She continued, slowly and deliberately emphasizing her words:

“Adaptive neuroplasticity, extension of active lifespan, increased resilience to age-related changes. These are very sensitive areas; each new technology requires careful, deliberate implementation. But we are confident: we are moving in the right direction.”

“Trials of new-generation mobile exo-armor complexes with adaptive power modulators have been completed.” Iris's voice remained even. “A network of tactical support drones has also been deployed, integrated into a scalable system. Soon, in a number of cities, Kairo's security service will work in close coordination with municipal structures, patrolling streets and ensuring response to emergencies.”

She nodded at the camera and concluded:

“This is not a random leap. We go where stability is needed. Our task is to continue the course that has allowed millions of people to live and work in a more understandable and predictable environment. We are not changing ourselves. We are maturing. The world changes—and we are already there to make those changes manageable.”

The screen went dark, and a resounding silence filled the room.

For several seconds, everyone just sat, as if trying to digest what was heard.

“Did you notice how she spoke?” Morozova said quietly, lifting her eyes. “Not a politician, not a techie… More like a perfectly trained orator. As if she knew: no one would dare object.”

“And no one will,” Vignon agreed, squinting. “When the country's entire logistics, medicine, and energy run under contract with Kairo, opposition is pointless. Better to nod.”

Anna frowned and added:

“This is no longer just business—it's the foundation of the state. And someone allowed her to become that. Not in one day.”

Tkach calmly set his cup on the table and, without shifting his gaze, coldly said:

“Or no one forbade it. And rightly so.”

Morozova stared at him, not expecting such directness.

“You mean you're not concerned?”

“Why should I be concerned?” Tkach shrugged. “In the last ten years, Kairo has stabilized the transport network, solved energy shortages, implemented digital medicine. And now it's taking on security. It's logical: where there is weakness, someone who knows how to act steps in. The problem isn't that the corporation is strong. The problem is that everyone else is weak.”

“You're justifying private concentration of power,” Morozova grumbled. “They patrol streets, manage chips in bodies, command drone fleets. That's a direct monopoly on force. Without elections, without restrictions.”

Tkach shrugged again:

“Yes,” he agreed. “But there's a result. Streets are safer, medicine works faster, infrastructure functions without failures. Maybe we just dislike that a corporation does the job better than the state. But that doesn't make it 'bad'.”

“As long as goals coincide,” Vignon interjected. “As long as interests don't diverge. You speak like an engineer, not a historian. All power systems first bring good—and then start setting their rules.”

“Or we're observing evolution,” Tkach argued. “The world is getting more complex, hierarchies change, classic institutions can't keep up with the speed. In their place come those adapted to the new world. Natural selection—nothing personal about it.”

“Nothing personal?” Morozova repeated. “And when they tell you your teaching license will be reviewed by 'cognitive efficiency coefficient for the corporation', you'll say that's natural selection too?”

Max closed his eyes. He listened attentively, as if an exposed current penetrated his consciousness. Deep down, he felt: a turn was brewing in the air. Something important was about to happen.

At that moment, Professor Blinov, without haste, set his cup on the table and quietly spoke. No word sounded in the room—everyone felt they should be silent.

“You know, colleagues… We always talk about power as if it's somewhere outside,” he began quietly, but each word cut the air. “As if it falls from above, descends from heaven, or appears by chance. In fact, it's something internal, inevitable. Power never drops suddenly. It's just redistributed.”

He slowly stood and, without lifting his gaze, paced along the window. As if the weight of his words gathered behind him.

“We call our society democratic. We talk about elections, rights, equality. But essentially… democracy is a historical anomaly, a brief interval when the balance of forces was accessible not only to the elite.”

The teachers exchanged glances.

“And do you know when it started?” Blinov continued, not looking at anyone in particular. “When gunpowder came into use. When any peasant with a primitive gun suddenly could kill a knight in expensive armor, raised and fed at public expense. Then equality became not a matter of status, but of tool, and power first became vulnerable from below.”

Morozova nodded, as if repeating the professor's thought:

“The gun gave the citizen political weight. From that moment, man became a subject of history.”

“And now,” Blinov continued, “we observe the opposite. Exo-armor, drones, artificial intelligence. All this is not democratic weaponry. It's not something you can just buy in a store or create in a shed. These are complex technologies requiring access, capital, infrastructure. This is a return to a monopoly—not feudal, but corporate.”

“The circle is closed,” Vignon summarized. “Again, power is where strength is available only to those who can afford it.”

“Yes,” Blinov nodded. “And the main thing: all this happens quietly. Not through war, but through development. People applaud, sign license agreements, tick the boxes in user agreements—and surrender everything, including the right to force, without a fight.”

Max listened with bated breath. It seemed the professor's words cut away everything superfluous, leaving only the naked structure of the world—with its nodes of power and flows of influence.

“So,” Tkach asked suddenly, “are we observing the death of democracy?”

“Not death,” Blinov answered calmly. “The end of one phase. Power changes form, but its essence remains the same: it's where there are opportunities, resources, control. And now—where technologies are.”

In that pause, no one dared to speak. Tkach stared thoughtfully into his empty cup.

“You, as always, are right, Vladislav Mikhailovich,” he said after a moment, quietly and respectfully. “Only you know… Historically, no state was created for the benefit of people. We came up with beautiful words later. At its core, power is strength. Who has the sword—has the law. The state is just the luckiest gang that managed to legalize its dominance. Then it puts on uniforms, writes laws—and robs the people by the rules.”

Morozova added:

“And now the sword is replaced by drones, implants, surveillance systems.” She smiled slightly, but in that smile there was no irony—only tense, anxious clarity. “In general, the same. Just a different interface.”

“And initially everyone is happy,” Vignon added. “There's order, medicine works, crime is lower. But we know: this is always just a stage. Before something new.”

Max averted his gaze to the cup, as if gathering words, and suddenly spoke quietly:

“Forgive me… maybe not the place… but you seem to have almost derived the formula.”

Everyone instantly turned to him. His cheeks flushed, but he continued—now more evenly, more confidently:

“Power is based on strength. Strength can be bought with money. Money is multiplied through power. It forms a cycle—money, strength, power… and back to money.”

The room fell into silence. In that pause there was either tension or quiet acknowledgment.

“Good approximation,” Tkach nodded.

“But not full,” Morozova said thoughtfully. “And what about faith?”

“Religious faith?” Vignon clarified.

“Any,” she replied quietly. “People submit not only because they can be forced. They submit when they believe that the one above them has the right to it. That he knows better. That he is really stronger.”

“And this is the most dangerous strength,” Tkach added. “It sometimes works even better than weapons. It's hard to buy directly… But if it's there, everything else may be unnecessary.”

“And vice versa,” Blinov pronounced calmly. “The loss of faith can bring down even absolute power.”

Anna smiled with the corners of her lips:

“And in nature it's the same. Before a fight, a cat arches its back to seem larger. It doesn't become stronger from that—but the opponent believes in her strength and retreats. Everything holds on perception.”

“Romans attached high crests to helmets,” Vignon added. “To seem taller, more impressive. Sometimes victory starts with the enemy beginning to believe in his defeat in advance.”

Slowly Blinov pushed away the cup and wiped his hands with a napkin. Then, as if performing a small ritual, he spread it before himself. He opened a small case, took out an ink pen. Max felt his heart thump dully: it seemed not just a thought was being born—a law was being formulated.

“We talk about strength,” the professor said quietly, “but strength doesn't arise from emptiness. It must have a foundation.”

He lowered the black tip of the pen to the napkin and carefully drew the first arrow.

“Money gives strength.”

Drew the second.

“Strength gives power.”

Closed the contour.

“And power returns money.”

He stepped back slightly and firmly pronounced:

M → F → P → M

“A closed loop,” he explained. “The more resources or money (M) you have, the more strength (F) you can afford. The more strength—the more power (P). And power, in turn, opens access to new resources. That's how a stable empire is arranged. That's how a corporation works. That's how a cult functions.”

He paused, then added unhurriedly:

“But even this scheme doesn't always work. It lacks one key multiplier.”

The professor wrote a new line under the first:

P = F × B

“This is the main secret,” he said softly. “Essentially, power is not just strength. Power is strength multiplied by belief (B). Formally, it is the product of strength and the belief of society, subordinates, supporters. And this is a simplified model… In reality, it's more complex, dozens of parameters can be added to this formula. As a first approximation, it works exactly like that.”

Max held his breath.

“The stronger people believe in the one at power,” Blinov continued, “the less real strength he needs. Sometimes it's almost not necessary to apply it: faith itself multiplies strength, makes it bigger than it is. Like invisible fire, it amplifies power. But if faith disappears—all power sooner or later crumbles to dust.”

The professor laid aside the pen. On the napkin before him, like a stamp of truth, stood out the formula of power.

Max looked at it, and scenes of all human history flashed in his head—from ancient leaders and kings to modern presidents and corporation heads. Everything fit into one pattern. Another blank space in his worldview had been filled.

Tkach, leaning back in the chair, first spoke, as if to himself:

“Today Iris Tanaka left no doubt about who holds power here.”

In the silence, weighed down by the realization of what was said, no one moved. The words seemed to take flesh: they lived here and now—on the streets of the city, in people's heads, in their fear and hopes.

A quiet delight stirred in Max’s chest—that very feeling when you suddenly understand: here it is, true knowledge. He felt he was in the right place, at the right time, where he could understand a lot and learn a great deal. It seemed fate itself led him to where he would finally get answers to his questions.

When the arguing teachers began to disperse, the napkin with the formula—forgotten by all—remained lying on the table. Max smiled barely noticeably. He carefully took the sheet, folded it twice, as if keeping a secret, and slipped it into his pocket, as if sensing that this knowledge would become a guiding thread in the darkest hour.
