Prologue: The Dream of the Old Lion

 

The room was silent, like a tomb. The ceiling light dissolved into the white walls. The air stood still—thick and sterile, each breath filtered, leaving only the hum of machines and a faint clinical scent of medication.    

He lay on the bed—no longer a man, but a statue of the past. Once a lion, a ruler, an architect of power. Now—a helpless body entangled in tubes. His breathing was mechanical and even, like a pump deep underground. His face was still. The veins at his temples throbbed faster—a fading echo of a departed will.    

She stood behind the glass.

When the door slid open softly, everything froze for a moment. Iris entered. Her black leather dress fit like molded armor. Her hair flowed over her shoulders like dark silk, her boots emphasizing a predatory, calculated gait. She moved like the apex of the food chain, and power itself—dense and tangible—filled the space, drawing everything into its orbit, pushing all thought aside.    

The doctor straightened to attention. He didn’t dare speak first, afraid to break her silence. His heart beat too loudly—the only reminder that he was still alive.

Iris approached her father. She watched him in silence, then sat on the edge of the bed and ran a gloved hand over his cheek—a gesture in which tenderness was only a shadow, hidden behind cold detachment. Her fingers trembled—either searching for a warmth they had never known, or sensing the emptiness consuming her from within. The price of strength.    

The veins at his temples throbbed faster—the rhythm of powerless fury, the last echo of former might.

“You always said that weakness is defeat,” her voice was level, even soft. “But now you are weak. You lie there, breathing through a tube. You are no longer a lion. You are a legend without claws.”

“You wanted to raise me strong. But you didn’t think I would become stronger than you. This strength pushed something else aside… I can’t remember what. Will you remind me? You created not a daughter—but a weapon. And that weapon is no longer in your hands.”    

She leaned close to his ear:

“I won.”

No triumph. No pity. No hatred. Only finality.

“I won’t kill you. That would be too simple. You will live. In this silence. Without power. Without a name. Until I decide otherwise.”    

She rose and headed for the door. The doctor hurried after her.

Halfway there, Iris stopped. She slowly turned to him and stepped closer. Her heel came down on his shoe—softly, precisely, like a seal pressed into wax. Not to cause pain, but to instill fear.    

She looked straight into his eyes. In their depths, something flashed—as if he saw his future sealed in her gaze—and he froze.

“You know what will happen if you let me down,” her voice was quiet, almost velvety.

“Y-yes… you can rely on me,” he muttered, turning pale.

Her gaze subdued him.

“Forget who he was. Forget what he meant. This is the end of an era.”    

As she left, she said, cold as a verdict:

“Let him live. Let him hear how I will rewrite history. No one will be able to object. Because I am the new reality.”
