Chapter 32. Good Boy

He slipped back into the dark.

Not sleep — oblivion .Pain had rooted itself deep in his nerves, spreading outward like frostbite, dragging him down into something thick and endless.No shape. No sound.Only cold.

Time no longer passed. His body no longer mattered.There was nothing left to feel.

Until —

A spark.A voice.

Soft. Hypnotic.Like music drifting through a wall, just faint enough to feel imagined.

Her voice.

Consciousness slammed back into him like a heartbeat returning from flatline.

And there she was.The face.

The most beautiful face in the world.

Perfect symmetry.Unsmiling lips — too flawless to be human.Eyes sharp and still and inhumanly calm .Like polished obsidian that had never known warmth.

She spoke.

“Someone damaged my people…”

Her voice flowed like silk across broken glass — delicate, terrifying in its control.

“They were wearing the most advanced armor we’ve ever developed. And still… they lost.Which means someone out there has something dangerous.A weapon that should not exist.”

She tilted her head, just slightly — the motion effortless, elegant, predatory .

“And you, Professor…Surely you know what it is.”

He tried to speak.His lips cracked. His throat was desert-dry.

But he forced the words out anyway.

“I… know nothing.”

She didn’t blink.

“Ah. Professor.”

Her voice didn’t rise, but it dropped a degree — enough for disappointment to curl around the syllables like a fine mist.

He inhaled with effort, breath catching against the pain.

“I’ve lived long enough,” he said.“Just kill me.”

She laughed.

Not loud. Not cruel.A low, affectionate sound — the kind a mother makes when a child says something adorably naïve.

“Oh, my sweet…”

She leaned in, and her gloved fingers ghosted across his face.Along the sharp lines of his jaw.Through the tangles of his sweat-matted gray hair.

“I’m not going to let you die.”

She was close enough now that he could feel her breath at his ear.Cool. Calm. Measured.

“There’s an entire medical staff here to make sure you don’t die. Not from pain. Not from exhaustion.Not even from despair.”

“I’m taking care of you.”

Her fingers kept moving — tender, exact, and terrifying.As if she were petting a pet she loved.Or a machine she owned.

“All you have to do is be…a good boy.”

He squeezed his eyes shut.

Her voice didn’t just speak anymore — it inhabited him. Slipped between thoughts. Took root.

He swallowed.

“Why…?” His voice was weak. But not trembling.“Why do you want so much power? Why take people’s freedom?”

She smiled — not cruelly. Not kindly. Just… knowingly .

“Oh, little fool.Freedom is a fairytale.”

“People have never been free.They’re ruled by hunger. By comfort. By chemicals in their blood.By gravity.They think they’re choosing — but they’re not.”

She straightened slightly. Her shadow stretched across his chest like a blade.

“Power isn’t the opposite of freedom.It’s its replacement.”

“Every social creature needs a leader.Without one, a pack disintegrates. It scatters. It dies.Humans are just a bigger, messier pack.And when power is weak, chaos creeps in.War. Plague. Collapse.So they give up their freedom — willingly — because they’re terrified of what comes without it.”

He looked at her, eyes aching, lips split.

“But who gave you the right to claim that power?”

“Natural selection.”

It was almost gentle. A whisper of fact.Like gravity didn’t need to be right. It just was .

“Efficiency,” she added. “That’s all this world rewards.And there’s nothing more efficient than a corporation.Why pretend otherwise? Why cling to myths, when the machine works?”

“People should have the right to choose.”

“To choose what?”

She stroked his hair again, smiling like a parent watching a child still believing in magic.

“To waste their lives?To chase dreams that will never matter?To fail , over and over, for the sake of some sacred illusion?”

Her voice softened further, now velvet — now poison.

“We set limits for children, so they don’t hurt themselves.Adults…aren’t so different.They want to be led.It’s not oppression.It’s evolution.”

“But what makes you the one who gets to decide for them?”

She tilted her head again. Something flickered in her gaze — something he hadn’t expected.

Not anger. Not cruelty.But genuine surprise.

“Haven’t you figured that out yet…?”

She didn’t wait for an answer.

Just turned.

The motion was seamless. She glided across the room, each step measured like choreography.No drama. No theatrics.

Only finality.

He swallowed hard.Something in his chest twisted.

“No…No, please… not that…”

She reached the door.

“Don’t leave me…”

She paused.

Just for a heartbeat.

But she didn’t turn around.And then she walked out.

The door whispered shut behind her.Like a sentence being sealed.
