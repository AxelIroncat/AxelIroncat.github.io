Chapter 3. Whisper of the Abyss

Blinov's laboratory was located in the most secluded wing of the old building, in a quiet dead-end corridor where even the echo of student life rarely reached. Upon their return, the sky had closed in with heavy leaden clouds, and the long corridor plunged into semi-darkness. The only sound was the hollow echo of their own footsteps, reverberating along the cold stone walls. Max tensed: the movement in the air had ceased, and even the slight vibration of their steps gave a sense of something important. The professor stopped at the massive oak door, covered with deep scratches and darkened by time. From his pocket, he took out a large, jingling bunch of rusty keys and unhurriedly inserted one into the ancient lock. The lock yielded with a prolonged creak, letting them inside. Max felt a barely perceptible quickening of his pulse: a premonition of something important mingled with burning curiosity.

When they entered, the laboratory was filled with semi-darkness and an expectant tension. On the walls, the reflections of standby indicators dully shimmered, and the air was thick with the smell of old circuit boards and wires. The ceiling lamps were off; the entire space was permeated only by the soft light of the instruments and the barely discernible hum of the equipment. The laboratory walls were hung with faded diagrams and blueprints, and tangled wires hung from the shelves like convoluted thoughts. Scientific life was bustling here, and under this sign, they met the changes that had long been circling this place. Max felt a tremor of excitement: as if all his secret aspirations had found their ultimate meaning here.

The professor first placed a small metal thermos and two ceramic mugs on the table. He lightly tapped the thermos lid with his finger, as if inviting Max to approach. Max, barely having crossed the threshold, froze in place. Something strange enveloped him from within, an almost instinctive feeling: "This is my place." His own desk with a computer stood here – not just a modest student refuge between lectures, but a full-fledged workspace for a researcher. Max ran his hand over the back of his chair, and a warm wave of belonging gradually spread throughout his body. His long-standing desire to understand how the world works had finally found its true home here.

The professor freed his hands and flipped a massive switch on the main distribution panel. Instantly, the laboratory hummed: the fans of old system blocks whirred, the lights of oscilloscopes flickered. Devices that had been dormant in the semi-darkness seemed to come alive in the light of the indicators.

Behind a screen in the depths of the laboratory stood a particularly strange apparatus. Its base was an old metal cabinet from a decommissioned spectrometer, peeling and chalk-marked; numerous panels with sensors were attached to it, and above them rose frames with thin coil windings. Dozens of multicolored connectors protruded from the sides, to which thick wire harnesses twisted intricately, like the roots of an unknown creature. Max involuntarily held his breath. The fact that he hadn't seen the setup before only intensified the feeling: it was not just an instrument, but a sacredly guarded secret.

"Is this it?" Max asked quietly, not taking his eyes off the strange apparatus.

The professor deftly poured hot tea from the thermos into the mugs, steam swirling above the cups. He nodded at Max:

"That's it. It looks like a patchwork quilt, but it works."

"And in short... what does it do?" Max asked, his eyes gleaming with enthusiasm.

"It tries to capture what our science has spent centuries learning to filter out," Blinov explained, crossing his arms over his chest. "We always considered all of this noise, interference, errors, to get to a clean, predictable signal. But what if we're wrong?"

Max listened, straining to hear every word from the professor. Suddenly, everything clicked in his head like a puzzle: he understood where this was leading.

"Because if we accept your hypothesis…" he muttered with growing excitement, "so many fields permeate space – gravitational, electromagnetic… Can it really be absolutely smooth?"

"Exactly!" a spark gleamed in the professor's eyes. "We are not listening to emptiness, Maxim. We are listening to this very 'noise.' Because it's not noise — it's speech. It just sounds in the language of space waves, which humanity does not yet understand. And we don't even observe these waves — we are immersed in them. They don't die down, but merge with the background — like ripples in an ocean where the listener himself is made of water. You and I, these walls, the lamp light — all of this is just stable patterns, ripples on the surface of this endless ocean."

"And if we manage to learn how to isolate these waves…" Blinov continued in a hushed tone, as if fearing the consequences of his words, "then, perhaps, one day we will be able to interact with them."

"Interact… with space?" Max whispered, as if afraid to disturb the fragile balance of the moment. The thought sounded insane. The metaphor of the wave growing into a tsunami, which the professor had spoken of that morning, resurfaced in his mind. A sacred fear — a strange mixture of admiration and horror — again ran shivers down his skin. It was like touching a power that should not be played with.

Blinov invited Max to the large screen. On it, a familiar graphic sparked — a pulsating signal, like a heartbeat. In the darkness of the laboratory, this pattern stood out particularly strongly, and a hidden rhythm could be discerned in the midst of this chaos.

"What's next? What's the next step?" Max asked softly, clenching his jaw in tension.

"For now, we study," the professor replied, leaning over the monitor. "No interference. Just listening. And learning to understand."

At that moment, a sharp, solitary spike unexpectedly appeared on the screen. Max straightened instantly — it took his breath away.

"Do you see this anomaly?" Blinov said, pointing a finger at the screen. "Before, similar spikes were attributed to interference or errors. But this one repeats. And it's by no means a coincidence."

Max leaned forward, his eyes widening in tense anticipation. The light on the graph faded, the spike disappeared — but then again, a little later, at the same frequency, it flared up anew. Blinov had already sat down at the computer, preparing to adjust the instruments.

"If we tune the analyzer precisely to this frequency, and the wave appears again," the professor murmured softly, without taking his eyes off the screen, "then we will get confirmation. We will begin to read this chaos."

Max held his breath. The analyzer settings appeared on the screen — thin filter lines immediately took positions on both sides of the expected signal. They stood like invisible guardians of the frequency, allowing only it to pass. Both froze in tense silence, no extraneous movement interrupting the stillness. Several agonizingly slow seconds dragged on… And suddenly, almost in the same place marked by the cursor, a sharp peak flared up again — even higher than before.

"There!" Max exclaimed. His throat was dry, his voice trembling with impatient joy. "This can't be a coincidence…"

Blinov involuntarily smiled, feeling his student's delight. But then he noticed: the professor's hand trembled as he adjusted his glasses. In his tired gaze, there was not so much the radiance of joy as a concentrated tension.

"Precisely," he quietly confirmed, shaking his head slightly.

Max's heart seemed to skip a beat, then pounded with renewed vigor. This was not just noise — it was a signal, regular and persistent. "A pattern..." the thought pierced him, "an ordered pattern, repeating with frightening accuracy."

The professor leaned back in his chair, closing his eyes to process what he had seen:

"We measure the Universe with instruments," he said almost in a whisper. "But we forget that they are created by hands that tremble with fear in the face of chaos."

The spike disappeared again. The graph drowned in the usual chaotic noise.

"We caught it… but we didn't hold it," Max sighed.

"We didn't even try to hold it," the professor replied calmly, shaking his head. "We just confirmed that it exists. That is enough for now."

Max felt a bittersweet relief: they had finally touched the truth, but it was clear how it was slipping away. He felt that the fiery triumph of victory was intertwined with the quiet anxiety of responsibility:

"We must…" a fire flared in Max's eyes, "we must tell everyone about this! Publish the results, share with the world — this will change everything!"

Blinov closed his eyes for a moment, gathering his thoughts. Then he said firmly:

"That's precisely why we won't show anything to anyone yet. Think about it: if we tell now — what will happen? Either people won't believe it, or, even worse, they will try to apply it without understanding anything. Truth, revealed prematurely, can destroy far more than it creates. The foundations of physics can collapse along with the accustomed world order. Any revolution requires preparation, and a scientific one — even more so."

The professor's words settled into Max's consciousness like heavy stones. The youthful pride of a discoverer swelled within him, but he forced himself to be silent. Max looked at Blinov's tired face and the solitary peak on the screen; the feeling of triumph was replaced by something else. His former fervor gave way to something new — cold-blooded wisdom. He realized that the professor entrusted him not only with this secret, but also with the weight of its burden.

"You have everything ahead of you, Maxim," Blinov said quietly, looking at his student with tired but warm eyes. "The main thing is not to lose that fire within. But learn to control it."

Max nodded slowly. He felt that this inner fire would indeed not go out. But now it would burn differently — not as a blinding flame consuming everything around it, but as a steady, focused light, warming from within.

He looked back at the screen. The solitary signal on it resembled the first star in the night sky: small, but bright and unwavering. The laboratory around him seemed to dissolve; only that light and the quiet hum of the instruments remained. Max carefully placed his palm on the cold metal casing of the mysterious apparatus, as if seeking a response within it. He felt a faint, barely perceptible vibration — as if invisible threads connected him to the device. It seemed to him that the very fabric of the universe breathed with the instrument, and his own heart beat in unison with it.
